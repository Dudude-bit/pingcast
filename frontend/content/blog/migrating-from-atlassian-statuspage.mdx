If you're paying Atlassian $29/mo (or $99, or $1499) for Statuspage and wondering whether you could migrate somewhere cheaper in an afternoon: yes, you can. The actual mechanical migration is one upload. The part that trips people up is what _doesn't_ transfer and why. Here's the whole thing.

## What Atlassian ships as a JSON export

In the Statuspage admin go to Settings → Advanced → Page Data → Export. You get a JSON file with four top-level sections:

- `page` — name, timezone, allowed subdomains
- `components` — every service on your status page, with a `status` per component
- `incidents` — every incident you've ever posted, with `started_at`, `resolved_at`, `name`, and a `components[]` list of affected services
- `incidents[].incident_updates` — the narrative timeline per incident, each with `status` (investigating / identified / monitoring / resolved / postmortem) and `body`

No subscribers. No analytics. No audience rules. Statuspage keeps those in their own tables and doesn't export them — by design, because CAN-SPAM requires fresh double opt-in when the sender changes.

## What our importer does

Drop the JSON at [/import/atlassian](/import/atlassian). One transaction:

**Components → monitors.** Every component with a URL becomes an HTTP monitor with a 60-second interval and `alert_after_failures=2`. Components without a URL (purely organisational groups like "Public API Services") are counted as skipped in the import result — we can't monitor an abstract grouping.

**Incidents → incidents.** Each one becomes a `is_manual=true` incident with `title` set from Atlassian's `name`, `cause` copied through, and `state` mapped directly (our state machine is a superset — `postmortem` collapses to `resolved` since we don't model post-mortem reports separately).

**Incident updates → timeline entries.** Every `incident_updates[]` row becomes an `IncidentUpdate` with the `state`, `body`, and `posted_at` preserved. Your full narrative history carries over verbatim.

The entire import is one database transaction. If any single row fails, nothing is written — so you either get a clean migration or a clear error message, never a half-migrated mess to clean up manually.

## What doesn't transfer (and why)

**Email subscribers.** Legal. CAN-SPAM / GDPR / 152-ФЗ all require a fresh double opt-in when the sender changes. We ship the subscribe form on your status page out of the box; the first time you post an incident on PingCast, your subscribers re-confirm. Painful for a week, then normal forever.

**Audience segmentation.** If you're on Atlassian Growth+ and send different incident updates to different customer segments, we don't have a 1:1 replacement yet. On the roadmap; not in v1.

**Your custom domain.** Easy to keep, takes one DNS change. After import, hit [/dashboard/custom-domain](/dashboard/custom-domain), enter `status.yourcompany.com`, follow the two-step instructions (set a CNAME, serve one token at `.well-known/pingcast/<token>`), and we handle the TLS cert. Takes ~10 minutes end-to-end once DNS propagates.

**SLA reports.** Atlassian's built-in SLA-by-component thing with uptime buckets is more polished than ours right now. We ship monthly uptime summaries + CSV export. For most indie SaaS that's enough; if you're on a contract that needs specific SLA math, stay on Atlassian.

## When it's not worth migrating

- You're on Enterprise ($1,499+/mo) with region-locked audiences and compliance attestations. PingCast can't match that yet.
- Your Atlassian plan is part of a bundle you're already paying for other reasons (Jira Cloud, Confluence, etc.) and the marginal cost of Statuspage is effectively zero.
- Everything's been stable for two years and nobody's asking for a migration. If it ain't broke, the ROI is marginal.

For everyone else — indie SaaS, early-stage startups, bootstrapped founders — it's 15 minutes of work for recurring monthly savings.
